FIORDLAND NATIONAL PARK

Explore More

Fiordland National Park, located in the southwest of New Zealand South Island, is a breathtaking natural wonder known for its dramatic fiords, towering mountains, and lush rainforests. It is home to famous fiords like Milford Sound and Doubtful Sound, offering stunning scenery and outdoor activities like hiking and boat tours. As a UNESCO World Heritage site, the park is rich in wildlife and pristine landscapes, making it a must-see destination for nature lovers and adventure seekers.

Top 3 Famous Wildlife in Fiordland


Takahe

Takahe is one ofThe new zealand's most iconic and rare birds.Once thought extinct,it has made a comeback thanks to dedicated conservation efforts. This fightless bird, with its large, vibrant plumage and striking red break , lives in Fiordland's aipine grasslands.the takahē was rediscovered in the 1940s.

Karearea(falcon)

The Kārearea, also known as the New Zealand falcon is a native bird of prey that is known for its agility and speed. Found throughout New Zealand, the Kārearea is a medium-sized raptor with a streamlined body built for swift flight, capable of reaching impressive speeds when hunting.falcons are also admired for their behaviour.

Dusky Dolphin

The dusky dolphin is a striking species of dolphin found in the coastal waters of the Southern Hemisphere, particularly around New Zealand. Known for its playful nature and acrobatic displays, the dusky dolphin has a sleek, dark-colored body with a distinctive pale patch on its sides, making it easily recognizable.

Fiordland: A cherished corner of the world.

Fiordland National Park is one of New Zealand’s treasured natural icons and is internationally recognised as part of the wider UNESCO World Heritage site, Te Wāhipounamu (Place of the Greenstone). It is home to glaciers, alpine ranges and unique flora and fauna that has been in existence since New Zealand was part of the supercontinent Gondwanaland.

Fiordland National Park itself covers 1.2 million hectares of mountain, lake, fiord and rainforest environments and is administered by the Department of Conservation. Human activity within Fiordland has been limited because of its challenging and wild landscapes.

Early Māori visited Fiordland for hunting, fishing and gathering takiwai (a unique form of New Zealand jade or pounamu). Much later, European sealers and whalers took shelter in the fiords and built a handful of small settlements, these being New Zealand’s earliest European settlements. But overall the sheer steepness of the terrain, the incredible isolation, and the wettest climate in New Zealand deterred all but the hardiest from settling in the region.
